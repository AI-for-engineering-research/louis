## crtm-inputs (Python side)

#22 — Validate particle scheme vs. NWP source compatibility

Some effective-radius schemes need HRRR-specific variables ERA5 doesn't provide. Wants a hand-written compatibility table so an incompatible NWP+scheme combo fails *early* with a clear message instead of crashing mid-computation. Must handle edge cases like HRRR data masquerading as an ECMWF-like dataset.

#21 — Always enforce (profile, layer/level) output format

Currently transposes datasets inconsistently (sometimes, not always), occasionally producing wrong output orientation. Should always check and transpose as needed. *(Related to the recent commit 2cb9a9d "Fix missing transpose to (profile, vertical)".)*

#20 — Refactor processing to batched/incremental writes

Today everything is interpolated and processed at once into one xr.Dataset, then written. Wants batched processing to bound memory and enable parallelization, with each batch written incrementally via netCDF4. Also suggests chunking (N_profiles, all layers) and optional low-level zlib compression. *(This is the Python-side mirror of the already-closed driver-side batching, #7.)*

## crtm-driver (Fortran side)

#19 — Chunk the driver's output NetCDF

Dominant access pattern is "one channel, all profiles," so chunk (profile, channel) → (N_PROFILES_IN_CHUNK, 1). Apply to brightness_temperature and radiance for both forward and K-matrix runs. K-matrix sensitivities likely (N_PROFILES_IN_CHUNK, 1 channel, all contrails), though contrail count is simulation-dependent.

## Cross-cutting

#18 — Tidy longitude-range handling

crtm-inputs converts everything to [-180, 180], but CRTM needs [0, 360] (and [-180, 180] for look-angle calc). Plan: inputs convert cleanly to [0, 360] and record it in the .nc attrs; driver expects that, warns on bad longitudes, and converts on-the-fly for look angles. *(Relates to commit 2538181 "Allow negative latitudes in look angles".)*

#17 — Update CRTM to 3.1.3

Submodule currently points at Ian's v3.1.0 fork (raises max vertical levels 200→400). Newer CRTM has fixes, notably a relative-humidity Jacobian fix. Two options: bump Ian's fork to v3.1.3, or point directly at JCSDA/CRTMv3 v3.1.3 (since the 400-level extension may be unused), accepting the risk of upstream history rewrites.

## The contract

It's a helper called once per output data variable, *inside NetCDF define mode* (before nf90_enddef). It assumes one specific layout convention that all our output variables follow: the profile dimension is always the last-defined dimension, and it's the unlimited one. The caller passes the chunk sizes for *only the leading (non-profile) dimensions*; the helper computes the profile span itself.

subroutine set_chunk_and_deflate(ncid, varid, fixed_chunks, varname)

  • fixed_chunks — chunk sizes for every dimension *except* profile, in the same order the variable was defined.

- radiance(channel, profile) → caller passes (/1/) (1 channel)

- t_sensitivity(layer, channel, profile) → (/n_layers, 1/) (all layers, 1 channel)

- contrail_iwc_sensitivity(contrail, layer, channel, profile) → (/contrail_dim_size, n_layers, 1/)

The 1 for channel is the whole point of the design — it's what makes "read one channel across all profiles" cheap.

## The two computed lines

Line 85 — how big is everything except profile?

n_other = product(fixed_chunks)

Multiplies the leading chunk extents into the element count of one profile-slice of a chunk. For t_sensitivity with 37 layers: n_other = 37 * 1 = 37. For radiance: n_other = 1.

Line 86 — how many profiles can one chunk hold?

profile_chunk = max(1, min(N_PROFILES_PER_CHUNK, TARGET_CHUNK_ELEMENTS / max(n_other, 1)))

Read inside-out, three guards stacked:

1. TARGET_CHUNK_ELEMENTS / n_other — the byte budget. We want a chunk to total ~TARGET_CHUNK_ELEMENTS (1M elements ≈ 4 MB of float32). Dividing the budget by the per-profile element count gives the largest profile span that stays within budget. This is the part that protects the high-dimensional K-matrix variables: with a big contrail × layer product, this term shrinks the profile span so a chunk never blows up to hundreds of MB. (Integer division, so it floors — fine here.)

2. min(N_PROFILES_PER_CHUNK, …) — the cap. Even if the budget would allow millions of profiles (e.g. radiance, where n_other=1 → budget allows 1,000,000), we don't want absurdly long chunks, so it's capped at 10,000.

3. max(1, …) — the floor. If a single profile-slice were itself larger than the whole budget, the division could yield 0; chunk sizes must be ≥ 1, so this clamps it.

The max(n_other, 1) in the denominator is just a divide-by-zero guard (product of an empty array is 1, but this makes it bulletproof).

Line 88 — assemble the full chunk shape:

chunksizes = [fixed_chunks, profile_chunk]

Array constructor concatenating the leading extents with the derived profile span, in definition order. So t_sensitivity becomes (/37, 1, 10000/), matching what ncdump showed.

## The two NetCDF calls

nf90_def_var_chunking(ncid, varid, NF90_CHUNKED, chunksizes)


nf90_def_var_deflate(ncid, varid, shuffle=1, deflate=1, deflate_level=DEFLATE_LEVEL)

  • NF90_CHUNKED sets explicit chunking with our computed shape.
  • deflate=1 turns zlib on at deflate_level=2; shuffle=1 enables the byte-shuffle filter, which reorders bytes by significance before compression and usually improves the ratio on floating-point data at negligible cost.

## Worked examples (matching what we verified)

| Variable | dims | fixed_chunks | n_other | budget term | profile_chunk | final chunk |

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| radiance | (channel, profile) | (1) | 1 | 1,000,000 | min→10000 | (1, 10000) |

| t_sensitivity | (layer, channel, profile) | (37, 1) | 37 | 27,027 | min→10000 | (37, 1, 10000) |

The budget term only *bites* when the leading dims get large — e.g. if layers were 400 and contrails 20, n_other = 8000, budget term = 125, so profile_chunk would drop to 125 instead of 10000, keeping the chunk near the 4 MB target instead of ballooning.

Short answer: for np.mod (and Python's %), the two are mathematically identical — the - 360 is a redundant no-op. But the distinction matters a lot in *other* languages, so it's worth being precise about why.

## Why they're the same here

np.mod(x, 360) uses floored modulo: the result always takes the sign of the divisor, so for divisor 360 > 0 the answer is always in [0, 360). Subtracting a whole period (360) before taking % 360 cannot change the result, because modulo is periodic:

(x - 360) mod 360  ≡  x mod 360      (for any real x, floored modulo)

Claude 19:59 UTC

Identical across the whole range. So between the two, mine is just the simpler spelling of the same operation.

## Why the - 360 idiom exists at all (and a caveat)

The (x ± 360) % 360 pattern is muscle-memory from languages where % is truncated (C, Fortran, JavaScript, Java), where the result takes the sign of the *dividend*. There, -130 % 360 gives -130, not 230 — so people add a period to force non-negativity:

| Expression | Python/numpy (floored) | C/Fortran/JS (truncated) |

|---|---|---|

| -130 % 360 | 230 ✅ | -130 ❌ |

| (-130 + 360) % 360 | 230 ✅ | 230 ✅ |

| (-130 - 360) % 360 | 230 ✅ | -130 ❌ |

Two things to note:

1. In numpy, none of the ± 360 adjustments are needed — np.mod(x, 360) already lands in [0, 360) for any input. That's why I wrote the plain form.

2. The specific form you quoted uses - 360, but the truncated-language safety idiom is + 360. (x - 360) % 360 would *not* fix the negative-result problem in C/Fortran (it still returns -130 for -130). It only happens to work in Python because floored modulo makes the offset irrelevant. So if the intent was "be robust to a different modulo convention," - 360 is the wrong sign — but moot here.

## Relevance to our code

This is exactly why the Fortran side of #18 (geometry.f90) uses modulo(lon, 360.0_fp) and not mod. Fortran has both:

  • mod = truncated (sign of dividend) → would leave -130.0 as -130.0. Wrong.
  • modulo = floored (sign of divisor) → gives 230.0. Correct, and the direct analogue of np.mod.

So: np.mod(lon, 360.0) (Python) and modulo(lon, 360.0_fp) (Fortran) are the matched, minimal, correct pair — and (lon - 360) % 360 would produce identical output in Python, just with a redundant term.
